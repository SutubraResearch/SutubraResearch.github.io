Sutubra Research is an energy system modeling practice that works with utilities, grid operators, and government agencies across North America.

Founded by Cameron Wade, based in Halifax, Nova Scotia. Cameron is also a Principal at Evolved Energy Research.

What Sutubra Does

North America is in the early stages of the largest build-out of electricity infrastructure in a generation. Load growth — from data centers, electric vehicles, building electrification, industrial expansion — has returned after decades of near-stagnation. Clean energy mandates are reshaping what gets built. And the weather patterns that underpinned a century of grid planning are shifting.

Sutubra Research builds the models that help planners navigate this. The work centers on a fundamental question that utilities, regulators, and grid operators all face: what should the electricity system look like in 20 or 30 years, and what needs to happen to get there?

Answering that means capacity expansion modeling — optimization models that weigh every combination of generation, storage, and transmission investment against cost, reliability, and emissions targets across a decades-long horizon. But a plan is only as useful as the assumptions behind it. Rather than producing a single optimal answer, Sutubra builds models that generate hundreds of near-cost-optimal alternatives — structurally diverse pathways that are all economically viable but look very different from one another. This maps the full landscape of what works: which investments show up in every scenario, which trade-offs are genuine, and which decisions depend on assumptions nobody can resolve today. It's the basis of a paper published in Nature Communications.

The same quantitative rigor extends to load forecasting. Climate change is reshaping extreme weather and the peak demands it drives, and the historical baselines that planners relied on are becoming less reliable. Sutubra builds probabilistic peak demand forecasts that account for these shifts — the output is a distribution of outcomes, not a single number, giving planners a realistic range calibrated to a climate that is no longer stationary. Cameron's data center load growth research, conducted through Carnegie Mellon's Open Energy Outlook, quantified how rapidly growing electricity demand could affect U.S. power sector emissions — work that was covered by the New York Times and led to a briefing with U.S. Senate staff.

The practice also models full-system decarbonization pathways — not just the electricity grid, but buildings, transportation, and industry — because the interaction between sectors is where many of the hardest planning questions are. And at the facility level, Sutubra optimizes distributed energy resources — sizing and dispatching storage, solar, and flexible loads to navigate tariff structures, grid service revenues, and uncertain load profiles. The same mathematical foundations that work at the grid scale apply here, just pointed at a different problem.

About

Cameron Wade is the founder of Sutubra Research and a Principal at Evolved Energy Research. His work spans capacity expansion modeling, demand forecasting, and decarbonization pathway analysis for utilities, grid operators, government agencies, and research institutions across North America.

His research has been published in Nature Communications, covered by the New York Times, and cited by U.S. lawmakers — including an invitation to brief Senate staff on data center grid impacts. He is a core team member of the Open Energy Outlook at Carnegie Mellon University, a co-investigator on the CANOE pan-Canadian energy model, and serves on the Technical Advisory Committee for Canada's Energy Modelling Hub.

Wade holds a BSc in Physics and Mathematics from Acadia University and dual MSc degrees — in Applied Physics and Mathematical Engineering — through the Erasmus Mundus programme. His early research included work at the European Space Agency's Advanced Concepts Team. He led energy systems research at the University of Victoria before founding Sutubra.
